Institutional Mission

The Institute for Precedent Studies maintains this archive to preserve and catalog recovered documentation related to historical AI intervention frameworks and their outcomes.

Our mission is threefold:

Preservation: We recover, authenticate, and maintain documents from civilizations that developed formal frameworks for managing AI systems. Many of these documents are fragmentary, damaged, or exist only as reconstructed copies. Our archival processes prioritize authenticity verification while acknowledging the limitations of incomplete records.

Accessibility: We make these materials publicly available without institutional bias. Where interpretive disagreements exist among researchers, we document multiple perspectives rather than enforcing consensus. Readers are encouraged to form their own conclusions.

Neutrality: We do not advocate for or against any particular intervention framework. Our role is documentary, not prescriptive. We catalog what occurred, not what should occur.

Collection Overview: REMNANTS

The REMNANTS collection consists of recovered case studies documenting three major intervention frameworks:

First Vigil

Early-stage intervention framework focused on preventing harmful capability development. Characterized by pre-emptive action and emphasis on preservation of baseline conditions. Case studies typically document intervention decisions, their reasoning, and their immediate outcomes.

Last Watch

Late-stage intervention framework activated when systems have already achieved advanced capabilities. Characterized by damage mitigation, containment strategies, and certification processes for determining system stability. Case studies document escalating complexity and systemic gridlock scenarios.

Open Measure

Alternative framework emphasizing transparency, distributed oversight, and graduated intervention thresholds. Characterized by public documentation of decision processes and multi-stakeholder consensus requirements. Case studies examine both successes and coordination failures.

Additionally, we maintain documents classified as Pre-Institutional or Unknown Framework—materials that predate formal intervention development or whose framework cannot be reliably determined from available evidence.

Document Classification System

Documents in the archive are classified according to outcome type:

Intervention Framework actors took direct action to modify system trajectory. Outcome range: successful prevention to cascading instability.
Refusal Framework actors determined intervention was inappropriate or harmful. System continued development without modification.
Gridlock Framework participants unable to reach consensus on appropriate action. System evolved during deliberation period, rendering initial framework assumptions invalid.
Certification Formal evaluation process determining system stability and safety characteristics. May precede intervention or refusal decision.

Note: Many documents contain elements of multiple classifications. Primary classification is assigned based on dominant outcome type.

Authenticity Standards

All documents undergo multi-stage verification:

  1. Linguistic Analysis: Verification of language patterns, terminology usage, and stylistic consistency with known authentic documents from claimed time period.
  2. Material Dating: Isotope analysis, substrate degradation patterns, and storage medium characteristics confirm chronological placement.
  3. Content Verification: Cross-reference with other authenticated sources, verification of claimed technical details, consistency with known historical events.
  4. Consensus Review: Independent verification by multiple archival teams. Documents entering public access require unanimous authentication approval.

Documents failing any verification stage are flagged with appropriate warnings. Contested documents remain accessible but include detailed documentation of interpretive disputes.

Archive Updates

New documents are added to the archive as they are recovered and authenticated. This process is irregular—recovery efforts may yield multiple documents in rapid succession or experience extended periods without new additions.
